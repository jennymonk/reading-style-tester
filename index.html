Reading Comfort Lab

Reading comfort is about how easy, tiring, clear or visually manageable text feels to read. Small changes in presentation can make a noticeable difference. A font that feels fine for one person may feel crowded or tiring for someone else. The same is true for spacing, contrast and background colour.

This site does not assume there is one perfect reading style. It is here to help you explore what works best for you.

Hello, I’m Jenny Monkhouse. I created Reading Comfort Lab because I experience visual strain myself and found it surprisingly hard to locate one straightforward place where I could test different fonts, spacing and background colours together.

There is a lot of helpful information online about dyslexia, visual stress, accessibility and readable design. What I struggled to find was a practical one-stop space where I could simply try different combinations and decide what felt best for me. This project grew from that gap.

It is designed as a calm, user-led tool to help people explore reading comfort in a simple and practical way.

This is not a diagnostic tool

Reading Comfort Lab is not designed to diagnose dyslexia, visual stress, Irlen syndrome, colour vision differences, migraine-related sensitivity, or any other condition. It is simply a practical tool for exploring combinations of font, spacing and colour.

If you regularly experience blurred text, moving words, headaches, glare or marked reading fatigue, it may be worth seeking professional advice as well as experimenting with presentation settings.

What can affect reading comfort?

Reading comfort can be shaped by lots of different things. Some are to do with the text itself, while others are about the person reading it or the environment around them.

✍️
Typography and layout Font style, font size, line spacing, paragraph spacing and line length can all change how easy text feels to track.
🎨
Colour and contrast Some people prefer softer contrast or a tinted background rather than dark text on a bright white screen.
💡
Light and screen conditions Glare, brightness, reflections and fatigue can make reading feel more effortful.
🧠
Individual differences Dyslexia, visual stress, headaches, migraine, concentration, tiredness and personal preference can all shape what feels comfortable.
